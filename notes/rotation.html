Step By Step

Current Cut Logic in Code

  • Session::decode_and_maybe_commit() in rust/bee-transcribe/src/session.rs decides when rotation should happen
  • DecodeSession::commit() in rust/bee-transcribe/src/decode_session.rs computes the cut, runs alignment, and returns committed vs remaining audio
  • TextBuffer::snap_to_word_boundary() in rust/bee-transcribe/src/text_buffer.rs is the current text-side boundary snap
  • DecodeSession::context_token_count() in rust/bee-transcribe/src/decode_session.rs tracks how much stable prefix is carried forward
  • SessionOptions.rollback_tokens in rust/bee-transcribe/src/types.rs controls the revisable tail

Interpretation

  • ROLLBACK is the unstable tail and stays revisable.
  • KEEP AS NEXT PREFIX is stable text retained for continuity in the next sub-session.
  • COMMITTABLE is the stable text eligible for finalization in the current rotation.
  • The final text cut is the boundary between committed text and carried-forward text, after snapping backward to a preferred textual boundary.

Alignment First

Proposed model: align the full current Qwen transcript against the full current audio once, derive timing for the whole stream, then apply the [C][P][R] partition on top of that timing.

Alignment-First Implementation Surface

  • full transcript comes from DecodeSession::text_tokens() in rust/bee-transcribe/src/decode_session.rs
  • Qwen decoding and confidence refresh live in DecodeSession in rust/bee-transcribe/src/decode_session.rs
  • the aligner choice is configured by Aligner in rust/bee-transcribe/src/types.rs
  • current code routes ZIPA alignment through zipa_word_alignments() in rust/bee-transcribe/src/decode_session.rs
  • current code routes Qwen timing through ForcedAligner from external crate bee_qwen3_asr, loaded in rust/bee-transcribe/src/lib.rs

Phone Alignment Mapping

The transcript side already carries structure. Qwen gives text and tokenization; G2P expands that text into phones; ZIPA gives phones from audio with timing. Dynamic programming aligns phone-to-phone, and that alignment can then be lifted back to token spans and word spans because the transcript-side hierarchy is already known.

Worked Example

Concrete example of the proposed alignment-first algorithm. The goal here is not exact token IDs or exact phone symbols; the goal is to show the lanes separately so we can reason about ordering, ownership, and where the cuts happen.

Stage 1 — Full current state before rotation

Suppose the current transcript is “turn on the kitchen lights please now”. The last word is still unstable, and we also want to keep a small stable prefix for the next session.

Stage 2 — Build transcript-side phones and audio-side phones

The transcript side already knows ownership: phones belong to tokens, and tokens belong to words. ZIPA gives timed phones from the audio side.

Stage 3 — Lift timing back to tokens and words

After phone alignment, each token and word inherits the span of the phones it owns. Now the text partition and the audio partition can be made consistently.

Stage 4 — Snap back to the preferred textual boundary

If the raw token-count cut lands inside a bad place, move the commit boundary backward to the preferred boundary, then use the timing attached to that boundary to trim audio consistently.
